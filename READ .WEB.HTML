The Whistling Man of Hollow Creek

In the autumn of 1987, the town of Hollow Creek had a population of 1,842 people.

By winter, it had 1,817.

Twenty-five people disappeared in three months.

No bodies were found.

No suspects were identified.

And no one could explain the strange whistling that echoed through the town every night. <>The first person to vanish was a fisherman named Arthur Bell. <>He left home before dawn as he always did. <>His boat was later found drifting in the center of the lake. <>The engine was still running. <>His coffee was still warm. <>But Arthur himself was gone. <>The sheriff assumed he had fallen overboard. <>It happened sometimes. <>The case was closed. <>Then a schoolteacher disappeared. <>Then a mechanic. Then a retired nurse. Each vanished under impossible circumstances. One woman disappeared while reading in her locked living room. Her book remained open on the couch. Her glasses rested on the page. The front door was still locked from the inside. The windows had never been opened. Yet she was gone. The town grew nervous. People started locking their doors earlier. Children were kept indoors after sunset. Businesses closed before dark. But the disappearances continued. Then came the whistling. At exactly 1:17 a.m. every night, a faint whistle drifted through town. It was always the same melody. Simple. Slow. Almost beautiful. No one knew where it came from. Yet everyone heard it. Some described it as distant. Others claimed it sounded like it came from directly outside their window. A few residents said something even stranger. They claimed the whistle seemed to move. As though whoever was making it was walking through town. Street by street. House by house. Looking for something. Or someone. Seventeen-year-old Daniel Carter first heard the whistle on November 12. He woke suddenly in the middle of the night. The melody floated through his bedroom. Soft. Almost hypnotic. He sat up and listened. The sound seemed to be outside. Across the street. Then it moved. Closer. His curtains shifted slightly. The whistle sounded directly beneath his window. Daniel felt an unexpected urge to look outside. An overwhelming urge. His hand reached for the curtain. Then his dog, Max, began growling. The sound startled him. Max had never growled before. Not at strangers. Not at animals. Not at anything. The dog stood at the foot of the bed with his fur raised. Staring at the window. Growling louder. The whistle stopped. Instantly. The silence felt wrong. Daniel slowly peeked through a tiny gap in the curtain. The street was empty. No people. No cars. No movement. Yet Max continued growling for another ten minutes. The next morning, Daniel told his father. His father turned pale. "Never look outside when you hear it." Daniel laughed. "You actually believe those stories?" His father didn't smile. "When I was your age, people disappeared too." Daniel stopped laughing. "What do you mean?" His father stared at his coffee. "There were disappearances in 1961." "Nobody talks about them anymore." "The whistling happened then too." Daniel felt a chill. "How many people disappeared?" His father looked toward the window. "Thirty-one." That night Daniel couldn't stop thinking about it. After dinner he visited the library. The librarian reluctantly helped him access old newspaper archives. Most articles discussed accidents, local events, and town meetings. Then he found something strange. A newspaper from October 1961. The headline read: SEVENTH MISSING PERSON THIS MONTH Another article: SHERIFF DENIES CONNECTION BETWEEN DISAPPEARANCES Then another. And another. By December there were dozens. The pattern matched exactly. The final article caught his attention. It contained a single photograph. A blurry image captured by a hunter. The photo showed a dirt road at night. Standing beside the road was a tall figure. Far too tall. Its arms hung below its knees. Its face appeared completely white. And beneath the image was a handwritten note. The Whistling Man. Daniel borrowed the photograph. The librarian tried to stop him. "You shouldn't take that." "Why not?" The elderly woman hesitated. Then whispered: "Because it notices people who look at it." Daniel laughed nervously. The librarian did not laugh. At 1:17 a.m., the whistle returned. This time Daniel was awake. Waiting. The melody drifted through the darkness. Far away. Closer. Closer. Max immediately began growling. The whistle moved down the street. House by house. Slowly approaching. Then something happened. The whistle stopped outside Daniel's home. Not near it. Outside it. Directly beneath his window. Max exploded into barking. The loudest barking Daniel had ever heard. The whistle remained silent. A full minute passed. Then two. Then three. Finally the melody resumed. Moving away from the house. Daniel waited another minute before peeking through the curtain. The street was empty. Except for something standing beneath a streetlamp. A figure. Tall. Unnaturally tall. At least eight feet high. Its body looked thin enough to snap in half. Its skin appeared pale gray. And its head was turned toward Daniel's window. Even though it stood nearly a hundred yards away. Daniel froze. The figure slowly raised one arm. And pointed. Directly at him. Then every streetlight went dark. Daniel screamed. When the lights returned seconds later, the figure was gone. The next morning another resident disappeared. A local barber named Frank Willis. His wife reported hearing whistling shortly before waking up alone. Frank's side of the bed was empty. The bedroom door remained locked. No signs of struggle existed. No footprints. No evidence. Nothing. Just another vanished person. Fear spread through Hollow Creek. People stopped leaving their homes after sunset. Churches filled. Gun stores sold out. The sheriff organized night patrols. For a few days things seemed calmer. Then three patrol officers disappeared simultaneously. Their abandoned vehicles were discovered on the edge of town. Engines running. Doors open. Flashlights still shining. No officers. No tracks. Nothing. That was when Daniel decided to investigate. His friend Emily volunteered to help. Together they mapped every disappearance. Locations. Times. Witness reports. Patterns. Eventually they noticed something. Every victim had heard the whistle shortly before vanishing. But some people heard it and survived. Why? What made the victims different? The answer appeared unexpectedly. In a journal written by a missing deputy from 1961. The deputy had become obsessed with the case. His final entry read: It only takes those who answer. Daniel reread the sentence. Again. And again. Answer. Answer what? Then he remembered something. Several witnesses had described hearing a second whistle. A reply. As though someone in town had whistled back. His stomach dropped. The victims weren't random. They had responded. That night Daniel and Emily stayed awake. At 1:17 a.m., the melody returned. Soft. Distant. Searching. The whistle moved through town. Then paused. A second whistle suddenly echoed from somewhere nearby. A reply. The original whistle stopped. The silence felt immense. Then came footsteps. Heavy footsteps. Moving through the darkness. Toward the source of the reply. The sound continued for nearly a minute. Then stopped. A scream shattered the night. The town erupted. Lights turned on everywhere. People rushed outside. The scream ended abruptly. The next morning another resident was missing. Now they knew. The Whistling Man was real. And it hunted those who answered. But Daniel still didn't know what it was. Or where it came from. He would soon discover the truth. And the truth was far worse than any ghost story. Because the Whistling Man wasn't merely killing people. It was collecting them. And beneath Hollow Creek lay a place where every missing person was still waiting. Alive. In the dark. And listening to a whistle that never stopped. To be continued...
